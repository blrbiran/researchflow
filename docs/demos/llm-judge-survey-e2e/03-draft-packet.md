## draft_text_or_path
### Draft prose
Large language models are increasingly used to evaluate generated text, model responses, and agent behavior because they can apply natural-language criteria at a scale that would be costly for human reviewers. High agreement with human preferences in selected settings has made this approach operationally attractive. Yet agreement alone does not establish that an evaluator is reliable, measures the intended construct, or remains trustworthy when the task, rubric, candidate order, or judged model changes.

This survey organizes LLM-as-a-judge evidence through five distinct lenses: construct validity, reliability, systematic bias, calibration and grounding, and governance. The separation matters because a judge may be repeatable but invalid, aligned with one annotator pool but biased under another ordering, or useful for screening while still requiring human adjudication for consequential decisions. By comparing evidence within this taxonomy, the survey aims to replace the binary question “Do LLM judges work?” with a conditional account of when their outputs can support credible conclusions.
